The Manual of City Building is the polar opposite of the Order of Numbers. It's a remarkably dry treatise that covers the practical considerations of founding, expanding, and governing a city. Its advice on handling water distribution is the most noteworthy divergent detail – it assumes water reservoirs can be located at any location convenient for the city, regardless of geographic realities, and repeats some specifications for grading channels to handle sewage with different wording an exhausting number of times, as though the reader might not understand why having waste water flow downstream and away might be desirable to begin with.

The passage on erecting city walls is not quite as tautological as the newspapers imply. The book has recipes for mixing mortar, techniques for estimating the strength of wooden beams, maps for digging canals, and pages of geometric formulae for arches, bridges, roads, and freestanding structures. It's no civil engineering textbook – it's missing the tables of material properties, and it doesn't have detailed instructions for tasks like building foundations – but both of those the book excuses by saying that local conditions and building materials will obviously vary, so the reader must confer with their master craftsmen to find out what concrete should endure when it's made from the nearest available supply of cement and aggregate, or how deep to sink foundations in a particular area. More importantly than all that: why does your city need a wall? Is it defending against wandering monsters, foreign invaders, rebellions, or some combination of the above? There are a few ways of handling pollution under various regimes, but the Manual of City Building suggests that if the city has a guild for the offending profession (undesirable but hard to avoid in practice) the prudent ruler has the guild post bond for pollution offenders so they are incentivized to police their own.
